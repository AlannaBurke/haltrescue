# Preparing for a Pet Sitter: A Comprehensive Guide

Traveling can be stressful, but leaving your beloved small pets in the care of a sitter doesn't have to be. Thorough preparation is the key to ensuring your pets stay safe and healthy, and your sitter feels confident and empowered. A well-prepared owner makes for a happy pet and a happy sitter.

## The Pet Care Bible: Your Sitter's Most Important Tool

Do not rely on verbal instructions. Create a detailed, written guide that contains everything your sitter needs to know. This "care bible" should be printed and left in a visible location.

### Section 1: Contact Information

*   **Your Contact Info:** Your cell phone number and the best way to reach you while you're away.
*   **Emergency Contacts:** At least two local friends or family members who have a key and can help in an emergency.
*   **Veterinarian Info:**
    *   Name, address, and phone number of your primary, exotics-savvy vet.
    *   Name, address, and phone number of the nearest 24/7 emergency animal hospital.
    *   A signed letter authorizing your pet sitter to seek veterinary care on your behalf, including a statement that you will be financially responsible for all charges.

### Section 2: Daily Care Checklist

Provide a clear, step-by-step checklist for each day.

*   **Morning Routine:**
    *   "Check on [Pet's Name] first thing. Are they bright and active?"
    *   "Provide fresh water. Bottle must be refilled daily."
    *   "Provide [X amount] of pellets."
    *   "Provide a handful of fresh hay."
*   **Evening Routine:**
    *   "Remove any uneaten fresh vegetables from the morning."
    *   "Provide the evening salad: [list specific veggies and amounts]."
    *   "Check water bottle again."
    *   "Spot clean any soiled bedding."

### Section 3: Detailed Feeding Instructions

Be specific. "A little bit of salad" is not helpful.

*   **Pellets:** Brand, exact amount per day (e.g., "1/8 cup of Oxbow Essentials Adult Rabbit Food").
*   **Hay:** Type of hay and where to find it. Emphasize that hay should be available 24/7 for herbivores.
*   **Vegetables:** A list of "Approved Vegetables" and the specific portion size per day.
*   **Treats:** A list of "Approved Treats" and the strict maximum amount (e.g., "One pea flake per guinea pig per day. NO other treats.").
*   **Forbidden Foods:** A clear list of what NOT to feed.

### Section 4: Health and Behavior

*   **Normal Behavior:** What does a normal, healthy day look like for your pet? "He sleeps in his hammock most of the day," or "She is very active on her wheel from 10 PM onwards."
*   **Subtle Signs of Illness:** This is the most important section. For a prey animal, what are the first signs they are unwell?
    *   "**For my rabbit:** Not eating his favorite treat is an emergency. Hiding more than usual. Small or no poop."
    *   "**For my hamster:** Not coming out of his burrow in the evening. Clicking sound when breathing."
*   **"When to Call Me vs. When to Go to the Vet":** Provide clear scenarios. 
    *   "Call me if he seems a little quiet." 
    *   "Go directly to the emergency vet if he has not eaten in 6 hours or is having trouble breathing. Do not wait to reach me."

## Prepare Your Supplies

Make everything as easy as possible for your sitter.

*   **Organize Supplies:** Place all food, hay, bedding, and cleaning supplies in one clearly marked area.
*   **Pre-Portion Food:** Use small plastic bags to pre-portion out each day's pellets and salad. This eliminates guesswork and ensures your pet gets the correct amount.
*   **Prepare the Carrier:** Leave the pet carrier out in a visible, easily accessible location in case of an emergency.
*   **Extra Bedding/Supplies:** Make sure there is more than enough of everything to last your entire trip, plus a few extra days in case of travel delays.

By taking the time to prepare thoroughly, you are setting your pet and your sitter up for a safe, stress-free experience. This peace of mind is priceless while you are away.

# Common Health Issues in Mice

Pet mice are charming and intelligent, but they are prone to several health conditions, many of which are unfortunately common and can progress quickly. Due to their small size and tendency to hide illness, a vigilant owner who performs daily health checks is their best advocate.

## 1. Respiratory Infections (Mycoplasmosis)

This is, without a doubt, the most common and significant health issue in fancy mice. Nearly all pet mice are carriers of the bacteria *Mycoplasma pulmonis*. While a healthy, unstressed mouse can often keep the infection at bay, any stress, poor husbandry, or old age can cause it to flare up into an active and chronic respiratory disease [1].

*   **Signs:** Sneezing, rattling or clicking sounds when breathing ("chattering"), labored breathing, a hunched posture, and sometimes a head tilt if the infection spreads to the inner ear.
*   **Management:** This is a chronic condition. While antibiotics from a vet (like doxycycline or enrofloxacin) can manage flare-ups, the infection is never truly cured. Maintaining a pristine, well-ventilated environment with low dust and no ammonia buildup is the key to keeping the disease suppressed.
*   **More Info:** [Respiratory Issues in Mice](./respiratory-issues)

## 2. Tumors

Tumors, especially mammary (breast) tumors, are extremely common in female mice. These can grow very quickly and, while often benign, can become very large, ulcerate, and impede the mouse’s movement and quality of life.

*   **Signs:** Any new lump or bump on your mouse’s body. Mammary tumors can appear anywhere from the neck to the hind legs, not just on the chest.
*   **Management:** Surgical removal is the only treatment. Because the tumors can grow so rapidly, it is important to have them evaluated by a vet as soon as they are noticed. Spaying female mice when they are young can significantly reduce the risk of mammary tumors [2].

## 3. Parasites (Mites and Lice)

Mice are susceptible to external parasites like fur mites and lice, which can cause intense itching and distress.

*   **Signs:** Excessive scratching, hair loss (especially on the back and neck), and visible scabs or sores from self-trauma.
*   **Treatment:** A veterinarian can diagnose the specific parasite and prescribe a safe and effective treatment, such as Ivermectin or Selamectin. Over-the-counter treatments are often ineffective and can be dangerous.
*   **More Info:** [Parasites in Mice](./parasites)

## 4. Dental Disease (Malocclusion)

Like other rodents, a mouse’s incisor teeth grow continuously. If their diet doesn’t provide enough hard material to gnaw on, or if their jaw is misaligned, the teeth can become overgrown.

*   **Signs:** Drooling, decreased appetite, weight loss, or visibly overgrown and curling front teeth.
*   **Prevention:** Provide a high-quality lab block diet and plenty of safe chew items like cardboard, apple wood sticks, or other wooden toys.
*   **More Info:** [Dental Health in Mice](./dental-health)

Because these conditions are so common, it is essential to find a veterinarian who is experienced with small rodents before you even acquire your pet mouse.

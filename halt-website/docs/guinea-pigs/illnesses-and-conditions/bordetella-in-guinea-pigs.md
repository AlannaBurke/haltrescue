# Bordetella in Guinea Pigs

*Bordetella bronchiseptica* is a bacterium that causes respiratory disease in a variety of animals. While it is most commonly known as a cause of "kennel cough" in dogs, it can be a life-threatening pathogen for guinea pigs. An infection can progress rapidly from mild respiratory signs to severe pneumonia, making it a true veterinary emergency. Understanding the risks, signs, and prevention strategies is crucial for every guinea pig owner.

## How is Bordetella Transmitted?

Guinea pigs can contract *Bordetella* from other animals, which is a primary reason why cross-species contact should be managed carefully.

*   **From Rabbits:** Rabbits can often carry *Bordetella bronchiseptica* without showing any signs of illness themselves. This makes them asymptomatic carriers that can easily transmit the bacteria to a guinea pig. For this reason, it is **not recommended to house rabbits and guinea pigs together** [1].
*   **From Dogs:** Dogs with kennel cough can easily transmit the bacteria to guinea pigs through respiratory droplets.
*   **From Other Guinea Pigs:** The bacteria can spread quickly between guinea pigs through direct contact or airborne droplets.

## Signs of a Bordetella Infection

A *Bordetella* infection in guinea pigs often presents as an upper respiratory infection (URI) that can quickly become severe. The signs can include:

*   **Labored Breathing:** Difficulty breathing, rapid breathing, or wheezing.
*   **Nasal Discharge:** A runny nose, which may be clear or colored.
*   **Loss of Appetite:** Refusal to eat or drink is a common sign of serious illness.
*   **Lethargy:** Weakness, hiding, and a lack of energy.
*   **Discharge from the Eyes:** Watery or crusty eyes.
*   **Coughing or Sneezing.**

**Any guinea pig showing signs of respiratory distress should be seen by an exotic veterinarian immediately.** The disease can be fatal within 24-48 hours if left untreated.

## Diagnosis and Treatment

A veterinarian will diagnose a respiratory infection based on a physical examination and clinical signs. A culture of the nasal or eye discharge may be taken to confirm the presence of *Bordetella*. Treatment must be aggressive and may include:

*   **Antibiotics:** Specific antibiotics that are safe for guinea pigs are used to fight the bacterial infection.
*   **Supportive Care:** This is critical and may involve hospitalization for fluid therapy (to combat dehydration), oxygen therapy (for severe breathing difficulty), and hand-feeding with a critical care formula to provide nutrition.
*   **Nebulization:** In some cases, a veterinarian may recommend nebulizing the guinea pig with saline or medication to help open the airways.

## Prevention is Key

Given how serious a *Bordetella* infection can be, prevention is the best strategy.

*   **Separate Housing:** Never house guinea pigs and rabbits together.
*   **Avoid Contact with Sick Animals:** Keep your guinea pigs away from dogs or other animals showing signs of respiratory illness.
*   **Quarantine New Animals:** Any new guinea pig should be quarantined in a separate room for at least 2-3 weeks to monitor for any signs of illness before being introduced to your existing pets.
*   **Good Hygiene:** Wash your hands thoroughly before and after handling your guinea pigs, especially if you have been in contact with other animals.
*   **Reduce Stress:** A clean, spacious habitat and a proper diet can help reduce stress, which supports a strong immune system.

By taking these preventative measures, you can significantly reduce the risk of your guinea pig contracting this dangerous infection.

Caring for a rabbit can be a deeply rewarding experience, bringing joy and companionship to your home. These intelligent and social creatures, capable of living up to 12-15 years, thrive with proper care and attention [1]. Understanding their unique needs, from diet to dental health and disease prevention, is crucial for ensuring a long and happy life for your furry friend. This guide will walk you through essential aspects of home health care for your rabbit, helping you provide the best possible environment.

## Creating a Safe and Stimulating Environment

Rabbits are naturally curious and require mental stimulation and social interaction to flourish [1]. While they can be litter trained, it's important to choose appropriate substrates. Ideal options include hay, soft recycled paper bedding, or shredded paper. **Avoid clay litter** as it poses a risk of intestinal blockage if ingested [1].

Proper handling is paramount to prevent injuries. Always support your rabbit's chest and hindquarters when picking them up to avoid spinal damage [1]. Unsupervised roaming, however, is not recommended. Rabbits have a tendency to chew on electrical cords and ingest carpet fibers, both of which can lead to serious health issues like intestinal blockages [1].

## The Importance of Spaying and Neutering

Spaying or neutering your rabbit is a critical step for both their health and responsible pet ownership. Beyond population control, these procedures significantly aid in behavioral management and reduce the risk of reproductive cancers [1]. An intact female rabbit can produce a staggering number of offspring—up to 14 kits per litter, potentially leading to 168 offspring annually [1].

## Nutritional Cornerstones: A Hay-Based Diet

The foundation of a rabbit's diet should be **unlimited high-quality grass hay** such as timothy, orchard, or brome hay [2]. This is vital for their digestive health and helps maintain proper teeth alignment, as rabbit teeth continuously grow [2].

### Dental Health

Chew toys are vital for maintaining dental health due to continuously growing teeth [2]. Providing a variety of safe chew toys can help prevent dental problems and keep your rabbit entertained.

### Digestive Health and Gastric Stasis

Coprophagy, the consumption of cecotropes (soft, nutrient-rich fecal pellets), is a normal and necessary behavior for rabbits to absorb essential nutrients like protein and vitamins B and K [2]. This process is a crucial part of their digestive system.

Gastric stasis is a common and serious condition in rabbits, often going unnoticed by owners because rabbits hide signs of discomfort as a prey species [3]. Symptoms include decreased food and water intake, leading to pasty gastrointestinal contents and dehydration [3]. Hair chewing can also indicate a low-fiber diet; a high-fiber diet and daily combing help prevent hairballs [3].

Treatment for gastric stasis involves aggressive fluid management (100–120 mL/kg/24 hr, potentially double for affected rabbits), pain medication (e.g., oxymorphone or hydromorphone), and motility stimulants (e.g., metoclopramide or cisapride) once the rabbit is hydrated and pain is managed [3]. It's important to note that oral force-feeding is discouraged if the GI tract is full or distended, and mineral oil and laxatives are ineffective for removing hair masses [3].

Prevention of GI stasis includes a high-fiber diet, stress reduction, obesity prevention, environmental enrichment, and daily grooming [3].

## Skin and Coat Care

Dermatological problems are common in rabbits, including scaling, crusting, nodules, tumors, odor, otitis, erosions, ulcerations, and nonhealing wounds [4]. Infectious causes of pruritus (itching) can manifest as hair loss, scaling, odor, and greasy skin [4]. Topical antimicrobial shampoos (e.g., 2% chlorhexidine/2% miconazole) can be used for skin infections [4].

### Nail Trims

Nail trims are typically needed every 4-8 weeks [3]. For light-colored nails, the quick (blood vessel) is visible, making trimming easier. For dark nails, backlighting with a flashlight can help locate the quick. If the quick is accidentally cut, styptic powder or cornstarch can be applied to stop bleeding [3].

## When to Seek Veterinary Care

Recognizing the subtle signs of illness in rabbits is crucial. Any significant change in eating habits, lethargy, or unusual behavior warrants a visit to a rabbit-savvy veterinarian. Early intervention can make a significant difference in the outcome of many conditions.
